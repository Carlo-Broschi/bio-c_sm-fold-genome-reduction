# Novelty and scoop check — bio-c (*The fate of the Sm fold under genome reduction*)

**Advisory only; no manuscript changes.** Verdict up front: none of the four claimed contributions (a)–(d) is pre-empted by 2023–2026 literature. The recent Hfq/Sm/Lsm literature is dominated by mechanistic and functional studies (Hfq–sRNA interactomes, RNA compaction, single-organism regulation); no genome-scale, completeness-controlled presence/absence census of the Sm fold across DPANN/Asgard/CPR has appeared, and no paper draws the bacteria-lose / archaea-retain asymmetry within one structure-guided three-domain phylogeny. Below, each claim is assessed against the closest recent work, with an explicit PRE-EMPTS / PARTIALLY OVERLAPS / COMPLEMENTARY tag, followed by recommended citations.

## Claim (a): archaeal Sm-fold retention

The manuscript already credits [Reichelt et al. 2018](https://doi.org/10.1042/ETLS20180023) as prior qualitative evidence and [Payá et al. 2023](https://doi.org/10.3390/microorganisms11051196) for broad Lsm retention across cultured archaea, claiming only the completeness-controlled, structurally-verified extension to reduced-genome MAG-dominated DPANN/Asgard lineages. Nothing since changes that framing.

The strongest recent context is [Li et al. 2025, *Trends in Microbiology*](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.tim.2025.02.004), a review of post-transcriptional regulation in archaea that cites Payá 2023. It is **COMPLEMENTARY**: a functional-regulation review, not a distribution census, but the single best recent citation for the archaeal-Lsm functional context and worth adding. In the same vein, [Payá et al. 2024, *IJMS*](https://doi.org/10.3390/ijms25010580) characterises Lsm-mediated sRNA regulation in the haloarchaeon *Haloferax mediterranei* (**COMPLEMENTARY**, single-organism functional study, same group as the 2023 census), and [Reichelt et al. 2023, *J. Bacteriol.*](https://doi.org/10.1128/jb.00280-23), already cited, reports *Pyrococcus furiosus* Lsm co-transcriptional binding (**COMPLEMENTARY**, correctly positioned). A more distant but interesting item is [Kim et al. 2025, *CSBJ*](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.csbj.2025.05.028), which uses AlphaFold3 to show that *Tevenvirinae* phage proteins adopt an LSm-like fold; it is **COMPLEMENTARY** and reinforces the same structure-based-homology logic bio-c uses, though it says nothing about archaeal distribution. No 2023–2026 study performs a completeness-filtered, Foldseek-verified Sm-fold census specifically across reduced-genome DPANN/Asgard genomes, so there is no PRE-EMPT and no PARTIAL OVERLAP on (a).

## Claim (b): two modes of Hfq loss

This claim has the most surrounding literature, but the surrounding literature is not the same claim. Recent Hfq papers ([Hfq–RNA interactome in *P. aeruginosa*](https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.2218407120); [RNA compaction and scanning, *Nat. Commun.* 2024](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41467-024-46316-6); numerous single-species regulation studies) are mechanistic, not distributional, and none is a census of Hfq presence across lifestyles keyed to genome size. The relevant recent items are reviews of the reductive-genome backdrop rather than of Hfq itself: [McCutcheon et al. 2024, *PLoS Biology*](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pbio.3002577) on how endosymbionts function with few genes, and the [Endomicrobia genome-reduction study, *mBio* 2024](https://doi.org/10.1128/mbio.00826-24). Both are **COMPLEMENTARY**: they frame the reduction gradient bio-c samples but do not track Hfq specifically.

The size-independent lineage-loss mode (Actinobacteria and *Myxococcus*, large genomes that nonetheless lack Hfq) is the sharper, less-anticipated half of the claim. The observation that Actinobacteria lack a canonical Hfq predates the search window, tracing to the classic [Sun, Zhulin & Wartell 2002](https://doi.org/10.1093/nar/gkf508) phyletic-distribution work already cited, but no recent paper frames Hfq loss as two distinct modes the way bio-c does. The whole claim is therefore **COMPLEMENTARY, with no PRE-EMPT**. One caveat for the author: because the per-lifestyle rates rest on a 71-genome stratified sample, a reviewer may ask whether the two-mode claim holds on an unbiased large-scale set; the manuscript already hedges this, and no published large-scale Hfq census exists to contradict it.

## Claim (c): total CPR loss

[Srinivas et al. 2024, *PNAS*](https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.2419369121), "Beyond genomics in Patescibacteria," is the current authority on CPR biology and their epibiotic lifestyle, and it does **not** examine Hfq or Sm-like proteins, which actually supports the manuscript's "first assessment" wording. It is **COMPLEMENTARY** and worth citing precisely at the point where the manuscript states these proteins had not been looked at. I found no study reporting Hfq or Sm/Lsm presence/absence in any CPR/Patescibacteria lineage, so there is **no PRE-EMPT**. This is nonetheless the strongest first-ness claim in the paper, and therefore the one most exposed if an unpublished or preprint census were to surface (see caveats).

## Claim (d): three-domain asymmetry synthesis

This is a synthesis claim, and synthesis claims are the hardest to pre-empt. No recent paper places bacterial repeated loss and archaeal retention of the Sm fold on a single structure-guided tree spanning all three domains. The closest cross-domain Sm/Lsm treatments remain older ([Veretnik et al. 2009](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pcbi.1000315), already cited) together with the qualitative archaeal reviews above. The claim is **COMPLEMENTARY, with no PRE-EMPT**.

## Recommended additions

Nothing forces a claim to be softened; the novelty structure is intact. The following are positioning additions, not retractions:

1. [Li et al. 2025, *Trends Microbiol.*](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.tim.2025.02.004), cite alongside Payá 2023 in §3.5 / Discussion as the current review of archaeal post-transcriptional regulation.
2. [Srinivas et al. 2024, *PNAS*](https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.2419369121), cite in §3.4 when asserting CPR proteins had not previously been examined; its silence on Hfq/Sm backs the "first assessment" wording.
3. [McCutcheon et al. 2024, *PLoS Biol.*](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pbio.3002577), optional, in §3.3 / Discussion for the endosymbiont reductive-evolution backdrop (a complement to Moran & Bennett 2014).
4. [Kim et al. 2025, *CSBJ*](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.csbj.2025.05.028), optional one-line Discussion point: independent structure-based evidence that the LSm fold recurs beyond cellular life, reinforcing the structure-guided approach.
5. [Payá et al. 2024, *IJMS*](https://doi.org/10.3390/ijms25010580), optional, for functional archaeal-Lsm support.

## Search caveats

OpenAlex indexes bioRxiv and the sweeps included preprint-eligible queries; nothing overlapping surfaced. Preprint coverage is never complete, though, so if a competing group has an unpublished Sm/Hfq-across-domains census it would most likely appear on bioRxiv, and a direct bioRxiv keyword check ("Hfq DPANN", "Sm fold CPR") close to submission is cheap insurance. Claim (c) is the most exposed first-ness statement; claim (a) is well-insulated by the explicit Reichelt and Payá credits; (b) and (d) are framed as quantitative or synthetic advances rather than first-detection, which is the safe framing. The recent Hfq literature is large but mechanistic, and triaging it by topic (distribution/evolution versus function/mechanism) left none of the functional work bearing on the distributional claims.
